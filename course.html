Unit 00 · Grades 5–12

Foundations of Inspiration

Before we create, we must learn to see. This unit is theoretical and philosophical — it challenges automatic perception and builds the conscious, active seeing that underlies all artistic practice. Students examine how the brain organises visual information, discover three modes of seeing, and practise shifting between them.

4 Lessons
Approaches: 🔭 Critical Looking 🧠 Visual Perception 📐 Gestalt Principles ✏️ Seeing Exercises 💬 Reflective Discussion
00.1
What is Inspiration
Moving from automatic perception to active, conscious seeing
Perception Active Seeing Consciousness
40 min
Inspiration is not a random occurrence — it is the result of a conscious act of seeing. This lesson challenges students to question automatic perception: we think we see, but mostly we recognise.
40 minutes
Ages 11–18
Theory + Discussion

00.2
How We See
Gestalt principles, visual organisation, and why we do not see reality directly
Gestalt Visual Cognition Perception Science
45 min
The brain does not record what the eye sees — it constructs an interpretation. This lesson introduces the Gestalt principles and shows how the brain organises raw visual data into meaningful patterns.
45 minutes
Ages 12–18
Theory + Visual Examples

Three Ways of Seeing
Analytical seeing · Emotional seeing · Conceptual seeing
Analytical Emotional Conceptual
45 min
There is no single way to look at a work of art. This lesson introduces three modes of seeing: Analytical, Emotional, and Conceptual. Students practise switching between modes when encountering the same work.

Active Seeing Practice

Observation Focused Practice Visual Filters

Unit 01 · Grades 5–12

Personal Experience as Inspiration

Your own life is the richest source you have. This unit moves through a clear progression: from the language of emotion, through line and composition, into memory and time-based journaling, arriving at an artist model and a final personal work. Each lesson builds on the last.

7 Lessons + Capstone Project
Progression: ❤️ Emotion ✏️ Line 📐 Composition 🧠 Memory 📖 Time 🎨 Artist Study ✦ Personal Work ✋ Form & Gesture
1.1
Emotion in Visual Language
Introduction — how does an inner feeling become a visible mark, colour or form?
Introduction Colour Abstraction Munch · Rothko
45 min Start Lesson
The opening lesson of the unit. Students encounter the central question: how does an inner feeling become a visible mark, colour or form? Through Munch and Rothko, they discover that colour is not decoration — it is emotional language. Students build their own abstract emotional palette as a foundation for the lessons that follow.
45 minutes
Ages 11–16
15 interactive screens · EN + עברית
Open Lesson
1.2
How Emotion Becomes a Drawing
Emotion → line quality → drawing → colour layer → personal mark
Line Colour Layer Van Gogh
55 min Start Lesson
The first studio lesson. Students choose a personal moment, identify its emotion, select a line quality that matches it (soft, sharp, broken, continuous…), draw using only lines — then add a colour layer on top. The process follows the real logic of working in layers: line first, then tone and colour, then a personal mark. 15 full-screen guided steps.
55 minutes
Ages 12–17
15 interactive screens · EN + עברית
Open Lesson
1.3
Emotion as Composition
How emotion spreads across the page — placement, space, and compositional choices
Composition Space Placement
45 min Start Lesson
After working with line in 1.2, this lesson shifts the question: how does emotion spread across the entire page? Students work with placement (where on the page), proportion (how much space), and the relationships between empty and filled areas. The focus is compositional decision-making as emotional expression — not decorating, but structuring feeling. 11 full-screen guided steps.
45 minutes
Ages 11–16
11 interactive screens · EN + עברית
Open Lesson
1.4
How Memory Becomes a Drawing
Memory → parts → composition → drawing → reflection
Memory Fragmentation Chagall
50 min Start Lesson
The shift from immediate emotion to memory. Memory is never complete — it arrives in fragments: a colour, a texture, a shape, a place. Students choose a personal memory, break it into its parts, draw the parts without trying to reconstruct a scene, then arrange them into a composition. Inspired by Chagall's dreamlike fragmentation. 12 full-screen guided steps.
50 minutes
Ages 12–17
12 interactive screens · EN + עברית
Open Lesson
1.5
How a Personal Journal Becomes an Artwork
Time-based — collecting a week of moments into a single page
Journal Time Collection Hundertwasser
50 min Start Lesson
Unlike 1.3 (which works with a single emotion) and 1.4 (which works with a single memory), this lesson works with time — a whole week. Students collect moments, things they saw, heard, read, wrote, felt — and place them together on one page without order. The result is a time-based composition: not one image, but an accumulation of living. 12 full-screen guided steps.
50 minutes
Ages 12–17
12 interactive screens · EN + עברית
Open Lesson
1.6
Artists as Inspiration — Frida Kahlo
How artists turn personal experience into art
Artist Study Symbolism Frida Kahlo
45 min Start Lesson
Students study Frida Kahlo's self-portraits as a personal visual world — figure, place, symbols, and color combined to express inner experience. They then create their own painting built from a personal experience.
45 minutes
Ages 13–18
15 interactive screens · EN + עברית
Open Lesson

✦ My Personal Story in Images Capstone
Final unit project — a visual sequence that tells a personal story
Final Project Series Portfolio
60+ min Start Lesson
The culminating project of Unit 01. Students create a 3–5 piece visual sequence drawing on everything built across the unit. Includes a short written artist statement and peer critique.
